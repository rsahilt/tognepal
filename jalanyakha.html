Jwalanhyaka Sinamuh
3D ORIGAMI AND QUILLING ART, SHOWCASING THE NEWARI HERITAGE.

Emperor Songsten Gampo of Tibet marrying the Princess Bhrikuti Devi of Nepal which was a portrayal of two cultures coming together. As The Lichhavi Princess Bhrikuti Devi of Nepal travelled to Tibet as the King’s bride-to-be, she was followed by a maid carrying "Jwalah Nhyaka" and "Sina Muh", a traditional Newari decorated hand-mirror and a vermillion (sindoor) container an important detail representing Princess Bhrikuti’s roots.

“Jwala” means fire and “Nhyaka” means mirror. It stands as a symbol of self-countenance, also considered as Divine Mirror encircled by flames. This object is specially used in the Newari tradition, in Nepal, during various rituals but mostly during Ihi (Immortal Marriage with Wood Apple, symbol of Lord Vishnu), Suryadarshan (Eternal Marriage with the Sun) and the Third wedding ceremony (non-divine marriage with the bridegroom).

“Sina Muh” is a part of the divine Newari mirror set, Jwala Nhyaka. Sina Muh is basically a decorated Vermillion (Sindoor) container covered by five-tiered parasol. A symbol of good fortune and prosperity in the Newar Community. This object is especially used in the Newari tradition, in Nepal, during various rituals but mostly during Ihi (Immortal Marriage with Wood Apple, symbol of Lord Vishnu), Suryadarshan (Eternal Marriage with the Sun) and the Third wedding ceremony (non-divine marriage with the bridegroom).


SUKUNDA
3D ORIGAMI AND QUILLING ART, SHOWCASING THE NEWARI HERITAGE.

A Newar's life is punctuated with religious ceremonies, every major event being auspiciously marked by a ritual. Sukunda is one of the elements that cannot be ruled out in any case to start the cultural tradition, rites and rituals. From a girl child’s entry into adolescence, a young boy’s bratabandha - coming of age ceremony, to marriage and eventually at death.

The sukunda is a hollow cup-like vessel that has a small projected plate at the top front that holds oil and a cotton wick to light. Since Lord Ganesha is worshipped before starting a journey or beginning a new venture, a sukunda which always has the impression of Lord Ganesh embedded into it backed by a five-headed serpent to provide shade to Lord Ganesh.

The projected plate symbolizes the Swayambhu Jyotir-Linga meaning the self-existent divine light. The lamp lighted symbolizes the great union of Shiva and Shakti. Lord Ganesh in the front represents great guru who is there to teach one and all about the supreme acts of God and his changeless inner nature. This is one of the reasons why Lord Ganesha is entitled to receive the first worship by his devotees before they begin any ceremony.

As per the Swayambhu Puran, the Kathmandu Valley was a large and deep lake inhabited by Nagas (Serpents). The lake was then cut drained by Bodhisatwa Manjushree with his sword, and the water was evacuated out from there. The five-headed snake hood over the Sukunda symbolize the Nagas, the original inhabitants of the legendary lake.

No socio-religious ceremony in the Newari community, big or small, ever starts until Sukunda is lighted and set up at the ceremonial spot. Lighting a sukunda is said to bring in good luck. The light from a sukunda is also believed to spread positive energy and to be a good omen. Therefore, a sukunda must be used at all Newari rites and rituals.
